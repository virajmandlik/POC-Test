Key Discussion Points
Context and Problem Definition
F4F supports smallholder farmers in India with agroforestry, aiming to generate carbon credits and share revenue with farmers. The onboarding process involves collecting and verifying multiple documents, currently handled manually by a data quality team.

Manual document checks are time-consuming and limit scalability.
Verification includes eligibility for carbon credits and KYC (Know Your Customer) processes.
Six key documents are collected per farmer; land records and photographic evidence are the focus for automation.
Automation Use Cases
Two automation use cases were detailed: (1) Verification of carbon credit training photos, and (2) Extraction of structured data from land records.

Photo verification requires checking for both a farmer and F4F representative in the frame, geotag, timestamp, and other metadata.
Land record extraction involves OCR of state-specific documents, with priority fields such as farmer name, location, and plot details.
Flagging of blurry or low-quality documents at upload is critical to avoid delays and repeated field visits.
Future extension to other document types (e.g., bank passbooks) is anticipated.
Technical and Data Requirements
The team discussed integration, technology preferences, and dataset availability.

Preferred API implementation is in Python/FastAPI; existing backend is PHP, with migration to AWS underway.
Sample datasets (including synthetic and anonymised data) will be shared for model development.
Support for Hindi and Marathi (Devanagari script) is required for OCR tasks.
Integration with F4F’s internal data collection app is planned; APIs should be modular and easily deployable.
Operational Considerations
The process for document upload and verification was clarified, along with privacy and field team workflow needs.

Documents are uploaded via shared drives or mobile data collection apps (e.g., Kobo server).
Immediate feedback on document quality at the point of upload is highly desirable.
Privacy concerns may limit inclusion of certain documents (e.g., bank passbooks) in the pilot phase.
F4F will handle backend integration and deployment post-API delivery.

Insights & Observations
The main operational bottleneck is manual document verification, which not only limits scale but also introduces delays and the risk of errors or repeated field visits. Automating quality checks at the point of upload (e.g., flagging blurry images) can significantly reduce operational friction and improve data quality.
The requirement to support both Hindi and Marathi (Devanagari script) for OCR is critical, as these are the primary languages in F4F’s operating regions. Existing open-source OCR models (e.g., Tesseract with language packs, or modern LLM-based OCR frameworks) could be adapted for this use case.
The flexibility to extend automation to additional document types (e.g., bank passbooks) and the modularity of the API design will future-proof the solution as F4F’s operations expand.
Actionable idea: Consider a two-stage pipeline—first, a lightweight image quality classifier (possibly using a small CNN or LLM-based vision model) to flag poor-quality uploads, followed by a document-specific OCR/extraction module. This approach aligns with F4F’s workflow and minimises unnecessary manual intervention.
